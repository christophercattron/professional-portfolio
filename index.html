ABOUT ME

I'm a professional engineer with a quirky sense of humor and a relentless drive for adventure, exploration, discovery, and creation.

With the COVID-19 pandemic, we are certainly living in unique times. After graduating with my M.S. in Civil Engineering in December 2019, I applied and was accepted to my dream graduate program: the SAHC Advanced Masters in Structural Analysis of Monuments and Historical Constructions—an advanced education program on the engineering of conservation of structures, with a focus on architectural heritage and buildings with cultural value. In September 2020, I was to begin coursework at the University of Minho in Portugal, after which I would complete a dissertation at the University of Padua in Italy. Unfortunately, due to circumstances surrounding the COVID-19 pandemic, I had to defer my enrollment to fall 2021. Between now and then, I am seeking a coop, internship, or temporary employment as a civil/structural engineer.

I was born and grew up in Saint Charles, Missouri, just 25 miles west of Saint Louis. I have a thoughtful, caring, intelligent wife named Maria, and together we have a mischievous cat named Daisy. In my spare time I have been learning HTML/CSS and JavaScript in part to build this website, and in part to gain computer and programming skills and understanding that I can apply in my professional career. Keep scrolling to learn more about my background, my experiences, and what makes me unique.

ABOUT THE LOGO

You may have noticed the blue “CC” logo in a few places on the website. Here’s a little more about the meaning behind the design.

The letters: CC is my initials—Christopher Cattron. The colors: the color palette is inspired by TRON. This is solely because you can’t spell Cattron without TRON. And blue is my favorite color :) The form: the CC is in the shape of a keystone, which is one of the most essential components of a voussoir arch. I chose the keystone shape due to my interest in historic structures. Since reading Jacques Heyman’s Stone Skeleton, I have been intrigued at the unique principles underlying the stability and safety/adequacy of unreinforced stone structures.
